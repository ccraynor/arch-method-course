Validate the Macro Performance Arc: Deriving and Testing What MRHN Learners Must Be Able to Do

Phase 1, Step 5
Scenario: Watch an expert work at Meridian Regional Health Network. The input is MRHN_CalibratedScopeMap_v1. The expert derives the end-of-course performance arc from the four buckets. Each bucket sub-arc faces three tests. One sub-arc fails the authenticity test and gets revised. The result is MRHN_PerformanceArc_v1 with governance record MRHN-D-004.

The MRHN team wrote a careful performance arc. So why does one of its four bucket sub-arcs still fail a test built to catch exactly this kind of error?

01

What Arc Validation Does, and Three Tests That Govern It

The performance arc is not a learning objective. Learning objectives describe what learners will do inside the course. The arc describes what learners will be able to do outside the course, in real MRHN incidents, when the course is over. The distinction matters because it sets the ceiling for decomposition. DC components must build toward independent, authentic performance, not just course completion.

Arc validation runs three tests in sequence. A sub-arc that fails any test is adjusted before decomposition begins.

Test 1 Is the arc internally consistent?
Coherence: does the arc statement hang together as a complete capability trajectory? A coherent arc has a clear starting point (what the learner cannot do now), a clear end state (what they can do after the course), and no internal contradictions. Incoherence usually appears as a vague end state ("understand incident response") or a gap in the trajectory.
Test 2 Does it reflect real MRHN practice?
Authenticity: does the arc describe what MRHN actually needs from its healthcare IT professionals in a real incident, or a generic, idealized version? This test applies the same lens as the domain authenticity scan in Step 4. Calibration annotations from MRHN-D-003 are the primary reference: any annotation that changed a scope item's interpretation must be reflected in the sub-arc that contains that item.
Test 3 Can this arc be reached in this course?
Feasibility: can healthcare IT professionals at MRHN reach this arc within the scope boundary (LC-1: 16 SI items) and under the operational constraints (PC-3 through PC-5: authority, communication, documentation)? An arc that requires capabilities outside LC-1 scope must be narrowed. An arc that requires competencies the constraint set does not permit must be annotated with a prerequisite note.
02

Derive the Macro Arc: What the Whole Course Must Produce

The macro arc is a single statement covering all four buckets. I derive it by reading across the bucket sequence: Threat Recognition and Triage, Containment and Control, Active Response Execution, Post-Incident Close-Out, and asking what a learner who can perform all four buckets competently can do in a real MRHN incident from start to finish.

The macro arc must pass all three tests before any sub-arc work begins. A macro arc that fails the feasibility test means the scope itself is too broad. That must be resolved now, not discovered in Module 3 stress-testing.

MRHN Macro Performance Arc

Given a confirmed or suspected cybersecurity incident at any MRHN facility, independently execute the appropriate Tier 1 or Tier 2 response protocol, including threat recognition and triage, containment and control, active response execution, and post-incident close-out, in compliance with HIPAA breach notification requirements, IR-2024 protocol constraints, and MRHN multi-site operational boundaries, producing complete documentation at each phase.

This arc passes coherence (clear start: cannot currently manage a cybersecurity incident; clear end: can independently execute the full response cycle), authenticity (names HIPAA and IR-2024 explicitly, names multi-site operational reality), and feasibility (bounded to 16 SI items, all within LC-1 scope; does not require capabilities outside PC-3 through PC-5 authority). I proceed to the four bucket sub-arcs.

03

Deep Dive: Validate the Bucket 1 Sub-Arc

I work through Bucket 1 in detail because it contains two Analysis-level scope items: SI-08 (Threat Identification) and SI-06 (Escalation Decision), and the sub-arc statement must capture that analytical demand. A Bucket 1 arc that reads like a procedure checklist will under-scope the decomposition for both items.

1 Bucket 1: Threat Recognition and Triage
Sub-Arc (first draft)

"Correctly identify and classify a suspected cybersecurity incident, assign a severity level using the MRHN rubric, and make the Tier 1 / Tier 2 escalation decision."

Coherence check: Pass. The arc covers the full Bucket 1 workflow from detection through triage decision with documentation implied by "classify" and "assign."

Authenticity check: Conditional pass: requires refinement. "Correctly identify" is ambiguous. For a trained incident responder it implies pattern-matching from experience. For MRHN healthcare IT professionals, SI-08 is calibrated at Analysis level because they must distinguish security incidents from system failures, user errors, and hardware problems without a practiced pattern library. The arc statement must reflect that distinction-making demand, not just "correct identification." Similarly, "make the Tier 1 / Tier 2 escalation decision" could be satisfied by consulting a lookup table. SI-06 is Analysis level because the authentic decision requires evaluating whether current team capability is sufficient, not just applying criteria. The arc must name that evaluation explicitly.

Feasibility check: Pass. All five Bucket 1 items are within LC-1 scope. The Analysis-level assignments are achievable for healthcare IT professionals over the course duration.

Sub-Arc (refined)

"Evaluate indicators of a potential cybersecurity incident to distinguish it from non-incident system events, apply the MRHN severity rubric to produce a documented severity classification, and evaluate whether Tier 1 containment capacity is sufficient, escalating to Tier 2 when indicated and documenting the escalation decision with criteria-based rationale."

Coherence: Pass Authenticity: Refined (Analysis demand explicit) Feasibility: Pass
04

Deep Dive: Validate the Bucket 3 Sub-Arc: Authenticity Test Fails

Bucket 3 is where the most consequential domain authenticity annotation lives: SI-10 (HIPAA 72-Hour Clock). The initial Bucket 3 sub-arc fails the authenticity test because it describes the HIPAA clock in generic terms that carry exactly the error the Step 4 annotation was written to prevent.

3 Bucket 3: Active Response Execution
Sub-Arc (initial draft: FAILS authenticity test)

"Execute the active response sequence including real-time incident documentation, leadership notification, and HIPAA breach notification clock management within the 72-hour window."

Why this fails: "HIPAA breach notification clock management within the 72-hour window" implies the clock starts when the breach is confirmed. MRHN-D-003 annotation for SI-10 is explicit: the clock triggers at "reasonably should have known" (45 CFR 164.404), not at forensic confirmation. An arc statement that uses "within the 72-hour window" without specifying the trigger point will produce DC components that teach the wrong start condition. If this arc travels into Step 6 unchanged, decomposition will produce instruction that creates systematic HIPAA non-compliance at MRHN.

Coherence check: Pass. The arc covers the full Bucket 3 workflow. The coherence failure is not the issue.

Authenticity check: Fail. Generic treatment of the HIPAA clock trigger. MRHN-D-003 SI-10 annotation must be incorporated. Additionally, "IR-2024 protocol deviation documentation" is missing from the arc entirely: SI-14 (RC-2 justification language requirement) is a Bucket 3 item and must appear. Two authenticity failures; both corrected in the revision.

Feasibility check: Pass (runs after revision: additional specificity does not add scope).

Sub-Arc (revised)

"Execute the active response sequence: real-time incident documentation, leadership notification per the PC-5 communication chain, management of the HIPAA 72-hour breach notification clock (evaluating the 'reasonably should have known' discovery trigger under 45 CFR 164.404), and protocol deviation documentation using RC-2 justification language when departure from IR-2024 protocol is required, producing complete records at each step."

Coherence: Pass Authenticity: Initially Failed Authenticity: Revised (two corrections applied) Feasibility: Pass
Notice this: the phrase "within the 72-hour window" sounds correct, yet it would teach the wrong HIPAA clock trigger.

This revision is the most important Step 5 decision in the MRHN arc. The "reasonably should have known" clause must now appear in every DC component that touches SI-10 in Step 6. The arc is the source of truth for that constraint going forward: the governance record for MRHN-D-003 noted the risk; the arc makes it permanent in the architecture.

05

Complete the Picture: Buckets 2 and 4

Buckets 2 and 4 pass all three tests with notes. I record the notes here because they constrain Step 6 decomposition for specific items.

2 Bucket 2: Containment and Control
Sub-Arc (validated)

"Apply MRHN-specific containment procedures: authority-bounded system isolation (PC-3), network segmentation with clinical system interdependency awareness, evidence preservation using the chain-of-custody procedure, and external notification per contractual and regulatory requirements, without disrupting patient care at active MRHN facilities."

The "clinical system interdependency awareness" clause reflects the MRHN-D-003 annotation for SI-09: network segmentation at MRHN requires evaluating EHR, imaging, and billing system relationships before applying segmentation procedures. "Without disrupting patient care" names the highest-stakes constraint that makes SI-09 Analysis level for this population. Both must carry forward into the SI-09 DC components in Step 6.

Coherence: Pass Authenticity: Pass (SI-09 annotation incorporated) Feasibility: Pass
4 Bucket 4: Post-Incident Close-Out
Sub-Arc (validated)

"Complete recovery verification against system restoration criteria, prepare and file the OCR breach notification with all eight required data elements (45 CFR 164.408), and produce a lessons learned document that distinguishes operational notes from the formal governed record subject to legal review, in compliance with MRHN-D-003 calibration constraints for all three items."

Two MRHN-D-003 annotations are explicit in this arc. The "eight required data elements" clause reflects the SI-07 annotation (45 CFR 164.408 requires specific enumeration, not generic "file a report" treatment). The "operational notes from the formal governed record" clause reflects the SI-13 Evaluation-level assignment: learners must judge what to document and where, not just conduct a retrospective. Both constraints must appear in the relevant DC components.

Coherence: Pass Authenticity: Pass (SI-07 and SI-13 annotations incorporated) Feasibility: Pass

MRHN-D-004 Performance Arc Validation Record
Decision
MRHN_PerformanceArc_v1 established as the ceiling for Step 6 decomposition. Macro arc and four bucket sub-arcs validated. Bucket 1 sub-arc refined to make Analysis-level cognitive demand explicit. Bucket 3 sub-arc required full revision: two authenticity failures corrected (HIPAA discovery trigger specificity; missing SI-14 IR-2024 deviation documentation). Buckets 2 and 4 passed with calibration annotation notes incorporated.
Constraint Reference
LC-1 (scope boundary: 16 SI items, no additions); PC-3 (system isolation authority); PC-4 (evidence handling); PC-5 (communication chain); RC-1 through RC-3 (documentation requirements); HIPAA 45 CFR 164.404 (discovery trigger definition); HIPAA 45 CFR 164.408 (OCR notification data elements); IR-2024 RC-2 (deviation justification language); MRHN-D-003 (calibration annotations, incorporated into revised sub-arcs)
Rationale
Bucket 3 revision was required because the initial arc described the HIPAA clock in generic terms ("within the 72-hour window") that would produce DC components teaching the wrong clock trigger. The MRHN-D-003 annotation for SI-10 identified this risk at calibration; the arc revision makes the correct trigger (45 CFR 164.404 "reasonably should have known") permanent in the architecture. Bucket 1 refinement captured the Analysis-level cognitive demand (evaluate and distinguish, not just identify) implied by the SI-08 and SI-06 taxonomy assignments.
Risk Note
  • Risk 1: If the Bucket 3 "reasonably should have known" clause is removed or paraphrased during Step 6 decomposition, the DC components for SI-10 will revert to the incorrect trigger. The clause must appear verbatim or by explicit cross-reference in SI-10 DC components.
  • Risk 2: If the Bucket 4 "formal governed record" language is softened, the SI-13 Evaluation-level assignment will be undermined and decomposition will produce Application-level instruction for a task with legal consequences at the Evaluation level.
Checkpoint Linkage
  • Step 6 Decomposition: each DC component must be traceable to the bucket sub-arc that contains its parent SI item. Revised sub-arc language constrains component content.
  • Gate 1.TG.1: MRHN_PerformanceArc_v1 is a required gate artifact. Arc must be complete and validated before gate review.
  • Module 3 Outcome Mapping: the macro arc is the benchmark against which assessment alignment is tested. Sub-arc specificity (especially Bucket 3 revision) is what makes that test meaningful.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

I run the three tests in order for a reason. Coherence first because an incoherent arc is not worth testing for authenticity. Authenticity second because an inauthentic arc may still be feasible, but feasibility of the wrong arc is irrelevant. I only run the feasibility test on arcs that have already passed the first two. This order prevents time wasted validating arcs that should be revised.

02

The macro arc is a single sentence (or a tight compound sentence). If I cannot state it in one coherent sentence, the arc is probably too broad. The macro arc is not a course description: it is the one thing a learner can do after completing the course that proves the course was worth taking. Everything in the course should be traceable back to this sentence.

03

"Correctly identify" is language that sounds precise but is not. It describes the outcome without specifying the cognitive process. For an expert, correct identification comes from pattern recognition. For a healthcare IT professional who has never managed a security incident, it requires active evaluation of competing hypotheses. The arc must name the process, not just the outcome, because the process is what instruction and assessment must target.

04

The Bucket 3 authenticity failure is not a surprising discovery: it was predictable from MRHN-D-003. The risk was flagged at calibration. Step 5 is the enforcement mechanism: a flag in MRHN-D-003 that does not appear in the arc is a flag that will be forgotten by the time a Module 2 designer builds the SI-10 DC components. The arc is how calibration constraints survive the handoff to decomposition.

05

The SI-13 constraint in the Bucket 4 arc is subtle. "Lessons learned document" sounds like one thing. The arc makes it two things: operational notes (freely documented) and the formal governed record (legal review required). A decomposition designer who doesn't see that distinction in the arc will build one component where there should be two, and miss the Evaluation-level demand entirely.

06

MRHN_PerformanceArc_v1 is the ceiling. Decomposition builds toward it, not past it, not short of it. Every DC component I write in Steps 3 and 4 of this lesson will be answering the same question: does this component, combined with the others, get a learner to the arc? If I cannot answer yes, the component should not exist. If a gap exists between the components and the arc, a component is missing.
