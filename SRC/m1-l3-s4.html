Quick Reference: Two Calibration Lenses
Lens A: Domain Authenticity "Would a generic course produce content for this item that is meaningfully wrong for MRHN's healthcare IT context?"

Pass: the item accurately describes the authentic task.
Flag: the item requires a calibration annotation to prevent generic treatment.
Lens B: Taxonomy Calibration "What is the actual cognitive demand for a healthcare IT professional who knows their systems but has never managed a cybersecurity incident?"
  • R: Recall: remember facts, definitions, prescribed steps
  • Ap: Application: apply a known procedure to a recognizable situation
  • An: Analysis: evaluate a situation, make distinctions, apply judgment
  • Ev: Evaluation: decide under genuine ambiguity; weigh competing constraints
How confident are you in your calibration judgments?

Success Criteria

  • You assign the taxonomy level that matches the real demand for healthcare IT staff who are not incident responders.
  • You write a clear annotation for every item you flag, so decomposition carries it forward.

Rationale
Domain authenticity scan identified five items where generic instruction would mislead Module 2 decomposition: SI-09 (multi-site MRHN network topology interdependencies), SI-10 (HIPAA discovery trigger misapplied in generic courses), SI-14 (IR-2024 deviation justification language requirements under RC-2), SI-07 (OCR notification requires 8 specific data elements under 45 CFR 164.408), SI-13 (lessons learned documents are discoverable in healthcare litigation). Taxonomy calibration for healthcare IT professionals elevated three items from their initial Application assignment: SI-10 to Analysis (legal reasoning required under ambiguous discovery facts), SI-13 to Evaluation (judgment required on legal exposure of documented findings), SI-09 to Analysis (clinical system interdependency evaluation required before segmentation). SI-08 and SI-06 assigned Analysis despite procedural names because this population lacks practiced pattern recognition for threat identification and escalation decision-making under incident conditions.
Risk Note
Risk 1 (Compliance): If the SI-10 domain annotation is not preserved into Module 2 decomposition, learners will be taught the incorrect HIPAA clock trigger, producing systematic non-compliance risk at MRHN when learners apply this skill in practice.

Risk 2 (Legal): If the SI-13 Evaluation assignment is downgraded to Application during decomposition, learners will not develop the judgment required to protect MRHN from litigation exposure in post-incident documentation.

Your Development

What your response reveals: your calibration choices show whether you can see past a task's label to its real cognitive demand. What to carry forward: when a scope item looks routine, test it against authentic practice before you decompose it.

Compare a miscalibrated item to a correctly calibrated one

Before
  • SI-08: Identify indicators of a potential cybersecurity incident, distinguish them from non-incident system events, and assign an initial threat classification.
  • Domain authenticity: Pass.
  • Taxonomy level: Application.
  • Annotation: Learners classify incidents, which is a routine procedure.

The procedural-sounding label "classification" drove an Application rating, with no annotation tying the level to this learner population.

After
  • SI-08: Identify indicators of a potential cybersecurity incident, distinguish them from non-incident system events, and assign an initial threat classification.
  • Domain authenticity: Pass.
  • Taxonomy level: Analysis.
  • Annotation: For this population, threat identification is not recall. Healthcare IT staff must separate real incidents from system failures, user errors, and hardware faults that produce similar symptoms. Without a practiced pattern library, they reason through ambiguous indicators rather than apply a known procedure. That reasoning is analytical, so the item is Analysis, not Application. Carry this annotation into Module 2 so decomposition builds reasoning practice, not a classification checklist.

The level now reflects the real cognitive demand, and the annotation will govern how the item is decomposed.

Why it is stronger

The strong version looks past the procedural-sounding label to the actual cognitive demand on this learner population and records an annotation that governs decomposition, while the weak version takes the label at face value and assigns a level that would teach the wrong skill.
