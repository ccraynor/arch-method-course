Reflection Checkpoint

Decomposition in Practice: What Would You Revise?

Reflection Prompt

The expert comparison on Page 4 was not built to give you a right or wrong score. It was built to make you pause. You saw a decision you made differently, and you had to judge whose reasoning is stronger. Now capture that analysis before the lesson ends.

  1. The decision: Identify one component decision from Page 4 that you would revise after seeing the expert decomposition. Name the SI ID and the specific decision: component count, DC identifier, taxonomy level, component description, or calibration constraint note.
  2. The revision: Describe what you would change. Be specific enough that another practitioner could execute the revision from your description.
  3. The reasoning: Explain what the expert comparison revealed that your original decision missed. Name the principle that governs the revision: calibration constraint, arc traceability, component independence, or taxonomy-level cognitive demand.

If your decomposition matched the expert pattern, identify the decision you were least confident about before seeing the comparison, and explain what confirmed or challenged your reasoning.

Writing Guidance

  • Name the SI ID and component number so your revision is precise, not general ("my SI-09 component 1 DC identifier" rather than "my first component").
  • For the revision, name what you would remove or change and what you would add or replace it with.
  • For the reasoning, name the specific principle, not "I was wrong" but "the calibration annotation required an evaluative demand I did not build in."
  • If your reasoning and the expert's differ and you stand behind yours, say so and explain why. Agreement with the expert is not the goal. Clear reasoning is.
