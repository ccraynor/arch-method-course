Purpose

Lesson 1.1b produced the Meridian Regional Health Network (MRHN) constraint set. It also locked the documentation conventions that govern all downstream artifacts. In this lesson you convert those 16 scope items (SI-01 through SI-16) into named, bounded categories. Those categories become the organizational skeleton of the course.

This is where the Bucket Map gets its name. A bucket is a named logical category that groups related scope items. Your Bucket Map records which scope items belong to which bucket and why. It also records whether the groupings are balanced enough to support the overall architecture.

Bucketing is not content organization in the usual sense. It is a structural decision. Your buckets determine how the course holds together through decomposition, sequencing, and stress-testing in Modules 2 and 3. Buckets built on weak rationale produce architecture that fails the balance check. Buckets built on constraint logic survive all the way to Structural Freeze.

Carrying forward from Lesson 1.1b: The constraint map (PC-1 through SC-2) and scope items (SI-01 through SI-16) from Steps 1 and 1.5 are the inputs to this lesson. The documentation conventions, including SI identifiers and the five-field governance rule, govern every decision recorded here.

What You Will Do

Step 2 Bucket Scope Items into Logical Categories

Review all 16 MRHN scope items and group them into named logical categories. Buckets are defined by procedural sequence, content type, or shared performance context, not by topic alone. Every scope item belongs to exactly one bucket.

  • Read all 16 scope items as a set before assigning any to a bucket
  • Identify natural groupings based on when and how each item appears in an incident response workflow
  • Name each bucket with a precise label that reflects its functional role, not just its subject area
  • Assign every SI-XX identifier to exactly one bucket with a written rationale
  • Record each bucketing decision using the five-field governance rule established in Step 1.5
Step 3 Evaluate Bucket Balance

With scope items assigned, evaluate whether the resulting buckets are appropriately balanced. Balance means each bucket is achievable within the project constraints and is sized appropriately relative to the others. Imbalanced buckets require documented adjustment.

  • Check each bucket against the binding constraint set: can this bucket be taught within the SME access cap, timeline, and delivery format?
  • Flag overloaded buckets: too many scope items, or content complexity that exceeds constraint tolerance
  • Flag thin buckets: items too sparse or too narrow to justify a standalone category
  • Evaluate whether bucket sequence reflects instructional prerequisite logic, not just content grouping
  • Document any re-bucketing decisions with constraint references and governance checkpoint linkage

What You Will Produce

Bucket Map and Scope Organization Package MRHN_BucketMap_v1_[Date]

The Bucket Map is the first major architecture artifact of the ARCH Method™. It organizes all 16 scope items into named, bounded categories. It also records the balance evaluation that confirms the groupings are constraint-compliant.

  • All 16 scope items (SI-01 through SI-16) assigned to named buckets
  • Your reasoning for placing each scope item in its bucket
  • Balance evaluation notes for every bucket
  • A record of any re-sequencing decisions you made and why
  • A documented sequence showing how each bucket builds on the one before it
