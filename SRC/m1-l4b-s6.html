Comparison Criteria

The calibration prompt asks you to do two things. Pick the one decision from your nine that you were least confident about. Then explain what extra information would have helped. Before you compare to the expert, use these criteria to judge what a strong response looks like.

  1. Item precision

    Names a single specific item by DC number and verdict, not a general category ("the optimization items were hard") or a method complaint ("the two-question test was unclear"). Strong calibration points to one item.

  2. Uncertainty source

    Identifies what specifically created the uncertainty: the component description wording, one of the Q1/Q2 test questions failing to resolve clearly, the adjacent component context (for Part 1), or the listed parent assignment (for Part 2). "I wasn't sure" is not an uncertainty source. The source is what produced the ambiguity.

  3. Information specificity

    Describes a specific piece of information that was missing, not a general request for "more context." Examples: "the full descriptions of other SI-16 components" or "the RC-2 document language itself" or "whether SI-16's arc constraint lists both facilitation and report production as co-obligations."

  4. Method connection

    Ties the information need to the specific optimization criterion (Q1 teachability, Q2 assessability) or the attribution method (subject matter anchoring by referenced standard). Strong calibration names the test, not just the feeling of uncertainty.

Expert Response

Review your decisions and the comparison criteria, then select Show Expert Response to compare.

My least confident decision was DC-47, the Split verdict. The conjunction "and" in "conduct the after-action review and produce the formal lessons learned report" is a reliable split signal, and both Q1 and Q2 fail on close examination. My uncertainty came specifically from Q1. Both activities belong to the same post-incident process: the review generates the findings, and the report formalizes them into the RC-4 governed record. I had to decide whether that sequential relationship made them one instructional unit or two adjacent units.

What resolved it: I asked whether a learner who had developed strong AAR facilitation skills but could not produce an RC-4 governed report would be considered competent for SI-16. The answer is no in both directions. A learner can become an effective MRHN AAR facilitator without ever producing the formal report. Those are organizationally separable roles. And a report writer can master the RC-4 format requirements without knowing how to run the review that generates the content. That directional independence is what Q1 is testing. The "same process" relationship makes them adjacent units that should be sequenced, not merged.

The additional information that would have most reduced my uncertainty: the full SI-16 arc language, specifically whether the arc constraint names "facilitate" and "produce report" as co-obligations within one scope item or as sequential obligations. If the arc constraint treated them as one obligation ("conduct and produce"), my uncertainty about splitting would have been higher. If the arc constraint listed them separately, the split would have been unambiguous.

For DC-38 in Part 2, the misattribution, my uncertainty was lower once I identified the subject matter anchor. When two SI items share the same bucket, level, and general documentation language, the referenced standard in the component description is the reliable distinguishing marker: RC-2 (protocol deviation justification language) belongs to SI-14; RC-3 (technical restoration thresholds) belongs to SI-15. DC-38 references IR-2024 protocol deviations and RC-2: that is SI-14 subject matter, not SI-15 recovery verification subject matter.

Why this response works

This response names a single item (DC-47, Split), identifies the specific test question that produced uncertainty (Q1, whether the sequential relationship makes them one unit or two), and names the specific information that would have resolved it (the SI-16 arc language and whether it treats facilitation and report production as co-obligations or sequential obligations).

The response also models what strong calibration looks like for DC-38: it names the subject matter anchor (referenced standard) as the resolution mechanism rather than describing a general comparison process. Strong calibration identifies the mechanism that resolves uncertainty, not just the verdict.
