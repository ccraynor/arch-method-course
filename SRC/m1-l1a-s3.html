Lesson 1.1a

Reading the Brief for Design Constraints

Scenario: Priya Okonkwo just sent the Meridian project brief. Now watch how an expert instructional designer reads it. In a few minutes, that expert pulls out four categories of information. Those four categories will govern every design decision that follows.

Most designers read a project brief looking for content. An expert reads it looking for constraints. The difference changes everything that comes after.

01

Identify the Learner Population and the Gap

I read the "Target Learners" section first, not because it is the most important part of the brief, but because I need a mental model of who I am designing for before anything else can be interpreted correctly.

The brief identifies approximately 120 healthcare IT professionals across three sites. Roles include systems administrators, network engineers, help desk staff, and IT managers. Prior knowledge is described as general IT competency with limited incident response training. All learners completed a HIPAA awareness module in 2022.

More important than the demographics is the capability gap the background section makes explicit:

That failure is my design target. I am not building a course for IT generalists who need to learn what a cyber incident is. I am building for people who already work in healthcare IT but cannot yet act independently in this specific, high-stakes procedural context under time pressure.

02

Read the Required Performance Items for Specificity

I read every performance requirement and note its specificity. Clients who write behavioral performance requirements are telling me exactly what the course must produce. The Meridian brief is unusually specific in three ways.

First, every item is protocol-specific. The requirement is not to "respond to cyber incidents." The requirement is to execute IR-2024: Meridian's internal protocol, adopted January 2024. That distinction eliminates a large body of generic incident response content I might otherwise consider including.

Second, the phrase "within their role authority" appears across multiple items. This tells me the course must address what different roles can and cannot do. A systems administrator has different decision authority than a help desk staffer. If I design a single uniform learner path without role differentiation, I will miss a performance requirement.

Third, item 5 specifies "Meridian communication templates." Not generic incident communication. Meridian's templates. This is often the last item on a client's list and the first to get cut from an underdeveloped scope. I flag it now.

03

Extract Every Design Constraint and Rank Them

Design constraints are not obstacles. They are inputs. I read the "Project Constraints" section methodically and I rank the constraints by their impact on scope decisions.

SME Access: 4 hours total with the IR team lead. This is the binding constraint. It ranks first.
Timeline: Delivery-ready within 90 days of project start.
Format: Self-paced online, SCORM 1.2. No synchronous delivery option.
Starting Point: IR-2024 protocol document only. No prior course content.
Budget: Fixed. No travel or in-person delivery.

Four hours of SME access for a course that requires demonstrated IR-2024 proficiency is tight. I cannot afford to use that time for content review and revision. I need scope locked and validated before the first SME meeting: which means Step 0 is not a formality for this project. It is a prerequisite for every hour of SME time I have.

The self-paced SCORM format also shapes my options for practice. No live cohort, no instructor feedback loop. Whatever assessment and calibration I design has to work without a facilitator. This raises the bar for scenario design.

04

Derive the Implied Out-of-Scope Boundaries

The brief does not include an explicit out-of-scope list, but the specificity of what is in scope allows me to derive one. Anything that is not covered by the client's stated learner population, performance requirements, and constraints is outside the boundary until the client says otherwise.

I document these boundaries immediately. The most expensive scope creep does not come from items the client asked for and I declined. It comes from items that were never discussed, arrived late in the project, and had no documented boundary to point to.

Out of scope: Clinical staff, administrative staff, and executive leadership training: the brief names IT professionals only
Out of scope: Policy revision or IR-2024 protocol updates: the course trains to IR-2024 as written, not as revised
Out of scope: Vendor and third-party coordination procedures: not referenced in the performance requirements
Out of scope: General security awareness content: the client explicitly rejected the compliance-checkbox model
Out of scope: Physical security and facility access procedures: not mentioned in the brief, not an IT professional domain

These out-of-scope items will appear in my written scope declaration in Step 0. Not as a defensive posture, but because the client deserves to know exactly what they are receiving and exactly what falls outside this project. A scope statement without out-of-scope boundaries is an incomplete scope statement.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

Notice the 2022 HIPAA awareness module. It tells me learners have regulatory framing but no incident response training. I will not build from zero on HIPAA. But I cannot assume any IR protocol knowledge whatsoever. The design gap is between "works in healthcare IT" and "executes IR-2024 independently under time pressure."

02

When a client writes "this is not a compliance checkbox course," they are rejecting a model they have seen before. I take that seriously. My performance tasks need to require demonstrated decision-making under realistic conditions, not multiple-choice recall. The specificity of these five performance items supports scenario-based design, which is consistent with what the client is asking for.

03

Four hours of SME time is the constraint that controls my approach to Step 0 most directly. If scope is wrong, I will discover it after the SME budget is spent. This is why scope declaration is not optional or approximate. I need a precise, bounded scope statement before I book the first SME meeting. The SME validates scope; the SME does not create it.

04

Documenting out-of-scope items is as important as documenting what is in scope. Both protect the client and the designer. In Step 0, out-of-scope items are not negotiating positions. They are explicit, shared agreements about what falls outside this project. If the client later decides an out-of-scope item belongs in scope, that becomes a visible scope change, with visible timeline and budget implications.

In a Different Context

A state education agency hires you to design reading instruction training for K-12 teachers. The brief lists dozens of reading strategies it wants covered. An expert sets the strategy list aside first and looks for the binding constraints: how much release time teachers actually get, which strategies the state assessment rewards, and what the agency can sustain after the grant ends. Those constraints decide which strategies make the course, long before any content is chosen.

In Your Context Think about a brief you received recently. What did you read first: the content list, or the constraints that would shape what was possible?
