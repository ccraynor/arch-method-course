Lesson 2

Design Document Synthesis

Scenario: In Lesson 1 you read the Meridian project brief for scope. Now watch an expert read the same document a second time. This time the goal is different. The expert extracts every constraint by type and ranks each one for design impact.

You already read this brief once for scope. What could a second read possibly surface that the first read missed?

01

Constraint Type 1 Performance Constraints

I read the brief a second time with a different purpose. The first read was about boundary: what is in and out of scope. This read is diagnostic: I am looking for conditions that constrain my design choices even within the agreed scope.

I start with performance constraints because they are the hardest ceiling. A performance constraint tells me what the learner must be able to do and how precisely. It is not a preference: it is a specification.

I extract five performance constraints from this section:

  • PC-1: Must execute IR-2024 specifically, not a generic incident response framework
  • PC-2: Must perform Tier 1 and Tier 2 decisions independently, without supervisor escalation
  • PC-3: Decision authority is role-bounded: different roles have different authority ceilings
  • PC-4: Must document in real time in Meridian's SIEM event log
  • PC-5: Must communicate using Meridian's approved templates, not improvised communication

PC-3 is the one I flag immediately. Role-bounded authority means I cannot design a single learner path. The course must account for what a systems administrator can decide versus what a help desk staffer can decide. That is a structural constraint, not a content choice.

02

Constraint Type 2 Regulatory Constraints

Regulatory constraints have an authority external to the client and external to me. Neither of us can negotiate them away. They set an absolute floor.

Three regulatory constraints:

  • RC-1: HIPAA 45 CFR 164.308(a)(5): workforce training is a covered entity obligation; the course must fulfill it documentably
  • RC-2: Must align to IR-2024 as adopted, not to a revised or idealized version of it
  • RC-3: OCR 18-month timeline: delivery must occur within the audit remediation window regardless of project preference

RC-3 creates a hard external deadline. The 90-day project timeline the client stated in the Project Constraints section exists inside that window, but the OCR timeline is the binding authority, not the client's preference. If the project slips, the OCR obligation does not.

Notice this: the deadline that governs is not the one the client wrote down.
03

Constraint Type 3 Logistical Constraints

Logistical constraints define what is buildable within this project. They bound the designer's options even when the performance requirements and regulatory requirements are fully understood.

Five logistical constraints, in descending order of design impact:

  • LC-1: 4 hours total SME access with the IR team lead: the hardest logistical ceiling
  • LC-2: 90-day delivery timeline from project start
  • LC-3: SCORM 1.2, self-paced online delivery: no synchronous option
  • LC-4: Fixed budget, no travel, no in-person delivery
  • LC-5: IR-2024 protocol document is the only existing content: no prior course materials

LC-1 controls almost everything else. Four hours of SME time for a course that requires demonstrated IR-2024 proficiency means the design must arrive at validation nearly complete. I cannot use SME time for discovery. LC-3 removes instructor feedback as an assessment option: whatever calibration I design must be self-contained.

04

Constraint Type 4 Stakeholder Constraints

Stakeholder constraints are client-stated conditions about process, quality, or approach. They are not externally mandated, but they are non-negotiable within this project without a documented conversation. Ignoring them is a project risk, not just a quality risk.

Two stakeholder constraints with different implications:

  • SC-1: Explicit rejection of the compliance-checkbox model: the client has named what they do not want, which constrains the assessment design toward demonstrated performance
  • SC-2: Architecture sign-off required before any content is written: no content development begins until the client approves scope and sequence

SC-2 is actually a process constraint more than a quality constraint. It means the Bucket Map and Scope Organization Package I am about to produce is not optional documentation: it is the gate through which content development must pass. That changes its priority in my work sequence.

05

Rank Binding Versus Negotiable

With all four constraint types extracted, I now rank them into two groups. A binding constraint cannot flex without a formal scope change, a regulatory waiver, or client sign-off on a revised agreement. A negotiable constraint can flex within designer judgment, but it must still be documented so the client knows what was decided and why.

This ranking is not permanent. If a binding constraint turns out to be wrong, if the OCR timeline was misread, if the SME hours can be extended, I update the record. But I start from the assumption that everything in the brief is binding until I have evidence otherwise.

Binding: cannot flex without a formal change
  • IR-2024 protocol alignment (PC-1, RC-2) Specified in both required performance and regulatory sections; changing it changes the course purpose
  • Role-bounded decision authority (PC-3) Appears across multiple performance items; drives the structural design decision about learner paths
  • 4 hours SME access (LC-1) Cannot be extended without budget and schedule change; determines when scope must be locked
  • SCORM 1.2, self-paced format (LC-3) LMS format specified; synchronous delivery explicitly excluded; removes instructor feedback from assessment options
  • OCR 18-month audit timeline (RC-3) External regulatory authority; does not move with project preference
  • Architecture sign-off before content (SC-2) Client-stated process gate; the Bucket Map and Scope Organization Package must be approved before development begins
  • HIPAA 164.308(a)(5) workforce training (RC-1) Covered entity obligation; training must be documentably completed
Negotiable: can flex within designer judgment, but must be documented
  • 4 to 6 hour learner-facing time estimate (LC-2 adjacent) The brief offers an estimate, not a ceiling; may increase if performance complexity requires it: requires client conversation if exceeded
  • Number and format of practice scenarios Not specified; designer professional judgment, constrained by SME time and development timeline
  • Instructional sequence within scope Not specified; designer's structural decision: subject to client sign-off on the architecture document

This ranked list becomes the spine of the Bucket Map. It is not a deliverable on its own: it is the working document from which the artifact is constructed. Every architecture decision in Modules 2 through 4 will be traceable back to this list.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

Reading a document twice is not redundancy: it is method. The first read establishes what the document is about. The second read extracts what the document requires of your design. Most novice designers do one read and attempt both tasks simultaneously. The result is incomplete constraint extraction and scope statements that feel complete but have invisible gaps.

02

The OCR audit timeline is the constraint most likely to be missed on a first read because it appears in the background section, not the constraints section. Clients do not always organize their briefs to surface every constraint in a single location. Design Document Synthesis requires reading every section for constraints, not just the one labeled "constraints."

03

The 4-to-6-hour learner estimate is listed as a constraint but it functions differently from the others. It is a client expectation, not a ceiling with regulatory authority. I note it as negotiable because performance complexity may legitimately require more time, but "negotiable" does not mean "ignore." It means the conversation must happen explicitly if I plan to exceed it.

04

"This is not a compliance checkbox course" is a design constraint, not just a sentiment. When a client names what they do not want with that specificity, it tells me they have experienced the thing they are rejecting. That rejection has direct implications: knowledge recall questions are not sufficient evidence of performance. The assessment design must require demonstrated decision-making under realistic conditions.

05

The binding category is deliberately longer than the negotiable category for this project. That is not a sign of a badly scoped project: it is a sign of a well-specified brief. A client who knows what they need and articulates it precisely makes the designer's job more constrained but less risky. Ambiguity is where projects fail, not specificity.
