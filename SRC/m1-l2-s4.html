Lesson 1.2

Evaluating Bucket Balance: Four Checks Against the Meridian Constraint Set

Step 3
Scenario: Meridian Regional Health Network -- the four-bucket map is established. An expert now applies four balance checks to each bucket: constraint compliance, item volume, instructional coherence, and sequence position. The three-item Post-Incident Close-Out bucket receives extended evaluation.
01

Define Balance Before Running the Checks

Balance does not mean equal. Four buckets do not need to contain four items each. An architecture with five items in one bucket and three in another can be perfectly balanced if both satisfy the same four criteria. The balance check is a constraint compliance test -- not a counting exercise.

For each MRHN bucket, I apply four criteria:

  1. 1 Constraint compliance: Can the bucket's scope items be delivered within the binding constraints -- LC-1 (4-hour SME access cap), LC-3 and LC-4 (SCORM self-paced, 90-day window), and PC-3 (role-bounded authority) where applicable?
  2. 2 Item volume: Is the number of scope items appropriate for this workflow phase -- neither overloaded (too many or too complex for the constraint budget) nor thin (too sparse to justify a standalone category)?
  3. 3 Instructional coherence: Do the items form a teachable unit? Do they share a performance context, prerequisite structure, or governing constraint that defines a coherent learning challenge?
  4. 4 Sequence position: Does this bucket sit correctly in the workflow sequence? Are the prerequisites it depends on established by the preceding bucket?

A bucket passes the balance check when it satisfies all four criteria. A single failure is a re-bucketing flag that requires a documented decision. I will note where flags arise and whether the re-bucketing test shows the flag is resolved or genuinely requires structural change.

02

Balance Check: Bucket 1 -- Threat Recognition and Triage

Bucket 1 contains five items: SI-08, SI-01, SI-16, SI-06, SI-02. Five items is the largest bucket in this set, which makes it the first candidate for an overload flag. The check begins here.

1 Threat Recognition and Triage
5 items -- SI-08, 01, 16, 06, 02
Constraint fit LC-1: triage and protocol classification is the highest SME priority -- IR-2024 Tier 1 and Tier 2 execution (SI-01, SI-02) and escalation protocol (SI-06) require expert review. LC-3/LC-4: scenario-based simulation is the appropriate SCORM delivery format for decision chains. Constraint budget is sufficient.
Item volume Five items is not overloaded here. The five items form a linear decision chain -- each activates the next. The cognitive load is sequential, not additive. Splitting this chain would require learners to reunite the prerequisite logic themselves.
Coherence All five items answer one question: what is this incident and am I the right person to handle it? Shared performance context, shared prerequisite structure, and a single governing challenge.
Sequence First position is correct. Every other bucket depends on threat classification and protocol activation. No upstream prerequisites exist.
Passes balance check. No re-bucketing required. Five items is appropriate for this workflow phase.
03

Balance Check: Bucket 2 -- Containment and Control

Bucket 2 contains four items: SI-03, SI-11, SI-09, SI-15. These are role-authorized operational actions within the containment window. The governing constraint for all four is PC-3 (role-bounded authority) -- a feature that reduces instructional complexity even as the content is technically demanding.

2 Containment and Control
4 items -- SI-03, 11, 09, 15
Constraint fit PC-3 governs all four items. Constraint coherence reduces instructional complexity -- learners apply the same authority boundary rule across all four actions. LC-1: containment is the second SME priority after triage. Constraint budget is sufficient for four items with shared governing logic.
Item volume Four items is well-scoped for the containment phase. Not overloaded (all governed by the same constraint) and not thin (each item requires distinct procedural knowledge: isolation, segmentation, evidence chain, external notification).
Coherence All four items are role-authorized physical or operational actions executed within the containment window. The shared learning challenge: execute the right containment action within your authority, without waiting for supervisor confirmation.
Sequence Second position is correct. Triage (Bucket 1) must confirm incident classification and activate protocol before containment begins. Hard prerequisite satisfied.
Passes balance check. Well-scoped. PC-3 constraint coherence supports instructional efficiency.
04

Balance Check: Bucket 3 -- Active Response Execution

Bucket 3 contains four items: SI-04, SI-05, SI-10, SI-14. These are concurrent obligations during active incident response. This bucket has the highest constraint density in the set -- four items with four different governing constraints -- which requires explicit attention in the constraint compliance check.

3 Active Response Execution
4 items -- SI-04, 05, 10, 14
Constraint fit Highest constraint density: SI-04 governed by PC-4, SI-05 by PC-5, SI-10 by RC-1 and RC-3, SI-14 by RC-2. Four regulatory or performance constraints across four items. LC-1 implication: each item requires SME review for compliance accuracy, particularly SI-10 (breach notification timing) and SI-14 (deviation from IR-2024). Constraint budget is sufficient but this bucket must receive priority SME time alongside Bucket 1.
Item volume Four items is appropriate. The concurrent nature of these obligations (all active simultaneously during response) means the instruction must teach learners to manage multiple obligations at once -- which is the correct difficulty level for this phase, not a sign of overload.
Coherence Shared temporal context -- all four obligations are active during the response window. The learning challenge: manage concurrent documentation, communication, regulatory, and compliance obligations without deferring any to post-closure.
Sequence Third position is correct. SI-04 (real-time documentation) can begin as soon as the incident is confirmed -- concurrently with Bucket 2 containment actions. SI-10 clock management begins at discovery. No items in this bucket require containment to be complete before they begin, though SI-05 (leadership communication) typically follows initial containment.
Passes balance check. Constraint density is high but manageable. Allocate SME time to SI-10 and SI-14 specifically. No re-bucketing required.
05

Balance Check: Bucket 4 -- Post-Incident Close-Out

Bucket 4 contains three items: SI-12, SI-07, SI-13. Three items is the smallest bucket in the set. That automatically triggers a thin-bucket evaluation. A thin bucket would require re-bucketing -- merging with Bucket 3 or restructuring the set. This is the most consequential balance check in the MRHN map.

4 Post-Incident Close-Out
3 items -- SI-12, 07, 13
Constraint fit SI-07 (RC-1, RC-3) requires SME review for OCR compliance accuracy. SI-12 is procedurally straightforward but high-stakes. SI-13 (lessons learned) is lower constraint density and can be largely self-directed. Estimated SME time: 30 to 45 minutes. Well within the remaining LC-1 budget after Buckets 1 through 3.
Item volume Three items -- thin bucket evaluation triggered. Three items is below the center of the range in this set (average: 4). This flag does not mean the bucket fails -- it means the thin-bucket test must be applied to determine whether the flag is resolved or requires re-bucketing. See evaluation below.
Coherence All three items require containment confirmation as a hard prerequisite. None can begin until the incident is formally closed and the full active-response record is available. The shared prerequisite is what defines this as a distinct phase -- not item count.
Sequence Fourth position is correct. Containment (Bucket 2) must be verified and active response (Bucket 3) substantially complete before any Bucket 4 item can begin. Hard prerequisite satisfied.
Volume flag raised. Thin-bucket evaluation required before verdict. Applying four-question test below.
Thin-Bucket Test -- Four Questions
1. Does each item require substantial procedural knowledge? Yes. SI-12 (recovery verification) is a multi-step integrity check. SI-07 (OCR breach documentation) requires precise compliance procedure. SI-13 (lessons learned) requires systematic retrospective analysis. None are one-step tasks.
2. Does this bucket have a distinct prerequisite that clearly separates it from Bucket 3? Yes. Containment confirmation is the hard gate: SI-12, SI-07, and SI-13 all require the incident to be formally closed before they begin. No item in Bucket 3 has this same prerequisite. The boundary is structurally clear.
3. Do these items fit within the remaining constraint budget? Yes. SI-07 needs 30 to 45 minutes of SME time. SI-12 and SI-13 require less. Estimated total: 45 to 60 minutes SME, well within LC-1.
4. Would merging Bucket 4 into Bucket 3 improve the architecture? No. Merging would blend concurrent-response obligations (Bucket 3) with post-closure tasks (Bucket 4) into a single instructional unit. Learners would encounter SI-07 (requires complete incident record) in the same context as SI-04 (real-time, begins at incident start). The temporal boundary that separates these obligations would disappear from the architecture. That boundary is one of the most important distinctions in incident response practice.
Volume flag resolved. Bucket 4 passes the balance check. Three items is appropriate for this workflow phase. The thin-bucket test confirms instructional substance, a distinct prerequisite structure, constraint compliance, and that merging would harm the architecture. No re-bucketing required.
06

Evaluate Sequence Logic and Record the Balance Decision

With all four buckets passing the balance check, the final evaluation is sequence logic: does the bucket order Bucket 1 to Bucket 2 to Bucket 3 to Bucket 4 reflect a sound instructional prerequisite chain, not just a content grouping?

Instructional Sequence and Prerequisite Logic
Bucket 1 Threat Recognition and Triage SI-08, 01, 16, 06, 02
Hard prerequisite
Bucket 2 Containment and Control SI-03, 11, 09, 15
Concurrent from incident start
Bucket 3 Active Response Execution SI-04, 05, 10, 14
Hard prerequisite (containment confirmed)
Bucket 4 Post-Incident Close-Out SI-12, 07, 13

Concurrency note: Bucket 3 items (SI-04 real-time documentation, SI-10 clock management) begin at incident discovery -- concurrently with Bucket 2 containment. The architecture sequence teaches them in order, but instruction within Bucket 3 must make the concurrency explicit. This does not require re-bucketing; it requires instructional design within the bucket.

All four checks pass. The sequence is sound. The balance check is complete and the decision is documented using the five-field governance rule established in Step 1.5:

Decision Record -- MRHN-D-002: Bucket Map Balance Check
1 Decision Statement
The MRHN course architecture will proceed with four buckets in the sequence established in Step 2: Threat Recognition and Triage (5 items), Containment and Control (4 items), Active Response Execution (4 items), and Post-Incident Close-Out (3 items). No re-bucketing is required.
2 Constraint Reference
LC-1 (4-hour SME access cap); LC-3 and LC-4 (SCORM self-paced, 90-day); PC-3 (role-bounded authority, Buckets 1 and 2); RC-1, RC-3 (OCR compliance, Buckets 3 and 4); SI-12, SI-07, SI-13 (Bucket 4 thin-bucket evaluation)
3 Rationale
All four buckets satisfy constraint compliance, appropriate item volume, instructional coherence, and sequence position. Bucket 1's five-item count is justified by the linear decision chain structure. Bucket 4's three-item count is justified by the thin-bucket test: distinct prerequisite (containment confirmation), sufficient instructional substance, constraint compliance, and the finding that merging with Bucket 3 would remove a structurally critical temporal boundary from the architecture.
4 Risk Note
If SME time is consumed disproportionately on Buckets 1 and 3 (both high constraint density), Bucket 4 item SI-07 may receive insufficient expert review before the 90-day delivery window closes. This risk is managed by establishing SME allocation in the project plan before decomposition begins in Module 2. A secondary risk: the concurrency between Buckets 2 and 3 must be explicitly addressed in instructional design during Module 2 -- if treated as strictly sequential, learners may not develop real-time multi-obligation management skills.
5 Checkpoint Linkage
Gate 1.TG.1 (Transition Gate, Module 1) will verify that all four buckets are constraint-compliant and that the Bucket 4 thin-bucket rationale is documented. Module 3 Outcome Mapping will confirm that the instructional content within each bucket prepares learners for the specific performance requirements of that workflow phase.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

The word "balance" sets the wrong expectation when designers first encounter Step 3. It sounds like counting. The four criteria make clear that balance is a fitness test, not an arithmetic one. A bucket with three items that all satisfy the four criteria is more balanced than a bucket with five items where two fail constraint compliance.

02

The reflex when seeing five items is to split. I have seen designers split the triage bucket into "classification" and "protocol execution" just to reduce the count. That split breaks the decision chain and requires learners to integrate the halves themselves. The five items are one decision sequence -- splitting them is an instructional harm, not an architectural improvement.

03

Constraint coherence within a bucket is a meaningful asset. When all four items in a bucket share the same governing constraint (PC-3), the constraint becomes the organizing principle of the instruction itself. Learners are not learning four separate things -- they are learning one governing rule applied across four operational contexts.

04

High constraint density does not mean the bucket fails constraint compliance -- it means the SME allocation decision matters more. Flagging constraint density here is a planning signal for Module 2, not a structural problem. The flag goes in the governance record where it will inform the SME meeting agenda.

05

The merge test in the thin-bucket evaluation is the one that does the real work. It is not enough to say "the three items form a coherent group." You have to ask whether the group is coherent enough to justify separate instructional treatment versus being absorbed into the adjacent bucket. When the merge would remove a critical temporal boundary from the architecture, the answer is always to keep the small bucket.

06

The governance record for a balance check decision feels like overhead when everything passes. It is not. The record captures the reasoning behind the thin-bucket decision for Bucket 4. When a reviewer in Module 3 asks "why does this course have a three-item post-incident module?" the answer is in MRHN-D-002, not in someone's memory.
