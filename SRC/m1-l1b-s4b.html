Faded Example: Apply the Output Style Gate

Step 1.5
How this works You just saw the Output Style Gate modeled in full. Now you apply the governance documentation rule to a new scenario. The first two fields are filled in as a model. You complete the remaining three. When you submit, the full expert version of all five fields appears. Each one carries a note on what makes it strong.

Scenario: Riverside Community College

Riverside Community College is developing a first-year experience course for underprepared students. Most students work part-time or have caregiving duties, many access coursework on a phone with unreliable internet, and most enter with significant gaps in college-level academic skills. The design team applies the Output Style Gate before any content is built.

  • LC-1 (Logistical): students average under 5 hours of weekly study time.
  • LC-2 (Logistical): mobile-first access with intermittent connectivity; no laptop or home internet can be assumed.
  • PC-1 (Performance): no prior college-level academic skills can be assumed as a foundation.

Complete the Governance Decision Record

Fields 1 and 2 are completed for you as a model. You write Fields 3, 4, and 5 for the same decision. Connect your Rationale to the constraints, name a concrete risk, and link the decision to a checkpoint.

What was decided, written as a precise, present-tense declaration. Example provided. Review and adjust if needed.
Which named binding constraint or identifier governs this decision. Example provided. Review and adjust if needed.
Why this decision follows from the constraints, not a restatement of the decision.
What could go wrong if this decision is incorrect or needs to change.
Which checkpoint will verify or revisit this decision.

Expert Version: All Five Fields

Compare your Rationale, Risk Note, and Checkpoint Linkage to the expert version below. Each field carries a short note on what makes it strong.

Riverside FYE: Delivery Structure Decision
1Decision Statement

The first-year experience course delivers each competency in short, self-contained modules that function on a mobile device without continuous internet access, and no module assumes prior college-level academic skills.

Why it is strong: a precise present-tense declaration of the design choice, not a goal or an aspiration.

2Constraint Reference

LC-1 (students average under 5 hours weekly study time); LC-2 (mobile-first, intermittent connectivity, no assumed laptop or home internet); PC-1 (no assumable prerequisite college-level knowledge).

Why it is strong: it cites specific named binding constraints, not a generic statement about student needs.

3Rationale

Each constraint independently forces this structure. LC-1 means content must be completable in short sessions, so long linear modules would go unfinished. LC-2 means delivery cannot depend on streaming or always-on access, so modules must work offline on a phone. PC-1 means no module may build on skills students were never taught, so each module must teach its own prerequisites. Violating any one constraint would make the course undeliverable for the intended learners.

Why it is strong: it ties the decision to each constraint and explains why the decision is necessary, rather than restating the decision.

4Risk Note

If later enrollment data shows most students have reliable devices and stronger academic preparation, the short offline-first structure could underuse available capacity and slow stronger students. The decision should be revisited if the verified learner profile differs from these assumptions.

Why it is strong: it names a concrete failure condition and the specific signal that would trigger a change.

5Governance Checkpoint Linkage

Verified at the Module 1 structural gate, where the learner profile and delivery constraints (LC-1, LC-2, PC-1) are confirmed before any content is developed.

Why it is strong: it names the specific checkpoint that will verify or revisit the decision.
