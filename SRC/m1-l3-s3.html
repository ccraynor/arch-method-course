Lesson 1.3

Domain Authenticity and Taxonomy Calibration: Preparing the MRHN Scope Map for Decomposition

Step 4
Scenario: Meridian Regional Health Network. MRHN_BucketMap_v1 is complete. Before decomposition begins, an expert applies two calibration lenses to each scope item: Domain Authenticity (does it reflect real MRHN practice?) and Taxonomy Calibration (is the cognitive demand appropriate for healthcare IT professionals who are not cybersecurity specialists?).
01

Two Calibration Lenses and What Each One Tests

Step 4 is not another pass over the bucket structure. The buckets are set. Step 4 examines what is inside each scope item -- whether the item as written reflects reality and whether the cognitive demand is correctly matched to this learner population. Both lenses can produce calibration annotations, and in some cases, adjustments that change how an item will be decomposed in Module 2.

A Domain Authenticity

"Does this scope item reflect how healthcare IT professionals actually perform this task under HIPAA and IR-2024 -- or does it describe a generic or idealized version?"

A domain authenticity flag means the scope item, as written, could mislead decomposition. The item may need a specificity annotation, a constraint reference, or in rare cases a rewrite.
  • Pass: item accurately describes the authentic task
  • Flag: item requires annotation to prevent generic treatment
  • Rewrite: item description is materially misleading (rare)
B Taxonomy Calibration

"What is the actual cognitive demand of this task for a healthcare IT professional who knows their systems but is not a trained incident responder?"

The taxonomy level assignment determines how complex the instruction and assessment must be. Four levels apply here:
  • Recall (R): remember facts, definitions, steps
  • Application (Ap): apply a known procedure to a recognizable situation
  • Analysis (An): evaluate a situation, make distinctions, apply judgment
  • Evaluation (Ev): decide under genuine ambiguity, synthesize across competing constraints

The learner population matters for every taxonomy assignment. Healthcare IT professionals at MRHN know their systems, understand HIPAA at a general compliance level, and have read IR-2024 -- but most have never managed a cybersecurity incident. Tasks that are Application level for a trained incident responder may be Analysis level for this population, because they must reason about what the procedure requires rather than recall it from practice.

03

Domain Authenticity: Three Items Examined in Depth

The three items that carry the highest calibration risk if left without annotation are SI-10, SI-07, and SI-13. Each represents a different category of authenticity failure.

SI-10 HIPAA 72-Hour Breach Notification Clock Management Bucket 3
Authenticity Finding
Definitional precision failure. The 72-hour clock is not a countdown from the moment a breach is confirmed -- it runs from "discovery," which HIPAA defines (45 CFR 164.404) as when the covered entity knew or reasonably should have known that a breach occurred. A covered entity that waits for forensic confirmation before starting the clock is already potentially out of compliance. Generic instruction that teaches "start the clock when you know it's a breach" will produce learners who systematically misapply this rule under real conditions.
Risk If Unannotated
Module 2 decomposition of SI-10 would build instruction around the confirmed-breach trigger. Learners would exit the course believing they have 72 hours from forensic confirmation. In an actual MRHN incident, they would begin the clock too late.
Calibration annotation: SI-10 must specify that the 72-hour clock triggers at the point of reasonable knowledge, not confirmed forensic verification. Decomposition must include a module on evaluating when "should have known" applies. This moves the item's authentic cognitive demand from Application to Analysis -- learners must evaluate discovery timing in ambiguous situations, not just start a timer.
SI-07 OCR Breach Documentation Bucket 4
Authenticity Finding
Procedural completeness failure. The item as written -- "prepare and file formal HIPAA breach notification with OCR" -- could be satisfied by any generic compliance form. The actual HIPAA Breach Notification Rule (45 CFR 164.408) requires eight specific data elements in the OCR submission: the nature of the PHI involved, the number of individuals affected, the date of the breach, the date of discovery, a description of what PHI was involved, a description of what the covered entity is doing to investigate and mitigate, and contact information. Missing any one of these elements creates a deficient notification. Generic instruction will not teach learners to check for all eight.
Risk If Unannotated
Decomposition would produce a "how to file a report" module. Learners would exit knowing that an OCR notification exists but not knowing what it must contain. The MRHN compliance team would have to correct deficient submissions after learners apply this skill in practice.
Calibration annotation: SI-07 must explicitly reference 45 CFR 164.408 and the eight required data elements. Decomposition must include a checklist-completion component and a scenario where learners identify which data elements they can populate from the incident record and which require additional documentation. This remains Application level but at higher procedural specificity than the item implies.
SI-13 Lessons Learned Documentation Bucket 4
Authenticity Finding
Healthcare-specific constraint failure. In most domains, lessons learned documentation is a straightforward retrospective exercise -- what went wrong, what worked, what to change. In healthcare, this document may be discoverable in subsequent litigation arising from the breach. Statements in a lessons learned document that acknowledge procedural failures can become admissions in legal proceedings. Generic retrospective framing will produce documents that MRHN's legal team cannot protect. Authentic practice at MRHN requires learners to understand what can be documented freely and what must be channeled through legal counsel.
Risk If Unannotated
Decomposition would produce a "conduct a retrospective" module. Learners would document procedural failures in unprotected records. The resulting documents could expose MRHN to legal liability in the event of patient- or regulatory-initiated litigation following a breach.
Calibration annotation: SI-13 must specify that the lessons learned document is a governed record subject to legal review before final filing. Decomposition must include a component on distinguishing between operational notes (for internal improvement) and the formal lessons learned record (which may have legal exposure). This elevates the item to Evaluation level -- learners must judge what to document and how, not simply conduct a retrospective.

05

Three Taxonomy Assignments That Required Expert Judgment

Most taxonomy assignments for this population are straightforward. Three required deliberate reasoning about the learner population and should not be adjusted during decomposition without a new governance record.

SI-08 Threat Identification and Classification Analysis -- not Application
Why Analysis
For trained incident responders, threat identification is Application: they have a practiced pattern library and apply it quickly. For healthcare IT professionals, identifying a security incident requires distinguishing it from a system failure, a user error, or a hardware problem -- all of which produce similar surface symptoms. This population must reason through indicators without a practiced pattern library. That reasoning is analysis.
Design Implication
Decomposition must include scenario-based practice with ambiguous indicators (not just clear-cut incidents). Assessment must require learners to explain their classification reasoning, not just produce the correct classification.
SI-06 Escalation Decision Protocol Analysis -- default-escalation risk
Why Analysis
Healthcare IT professionals without incident response experience have a strong default toward escalating everything -- it feels like the safe choice. But unnecessary escalation delays Tier 1 response, consumes leadership bandwidth, and creates confusion about who has authority. The authentic decision requires evaluating whether current team capability is sufficient to contain without escalation. That is an analytical judgment, not a procedure lookup.
Design Implication
Decomposition must include practice scenarios where the correct answer is to NOT escalate. Assessment must distinguish between learners who apply the escalation criteria and learners who escalate reflexively.
SI-09 Network Segmentation Procedures Analysis -- system interdependency risk
Why Analysis
At MRHN, network segmentation is not a generic IT procedure. The multi-site infrastructure includes EHR systems with patient care dependencies, medical imaging systems, and billing systems -- all with different availability requirements. Disconnecting a segment that carries real-time clinical data can affect patient care. Learners must analyze system interdependencies before applying segmentation, even under pressure. This is an MRHN-specific constraint that makes a standard Application task into an Analysis task for this context.
Design Implication
Decomposition must include MRHN-specific network topology context and scenarios that involve clinical availability tradeoffs. This is where the domain authenticity annotation for SI-09 directly shapes the taxonomy assignment. Both flags are linked.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

The learner population specification is not background information -- it is the input to the taxonomy calibration. The same scope item carries a different taxonomy level for an experienced incident responder than for a healthcare IT professional applying the procedure for the first time. If you calibrate for the wrong population, you will design instruction that is either too shallow or too demanding for the people in the room.

02

The domain authenticity scan is a fast read, not a deep analysis. I am asking one question per item: would a generic course get this wrong? Most items pass because incident response procedures are largely consistent across healthcare and general cybersecurity. The flags concentrate in the regulatory-compliance items (SI-10, SI-07) and the healthcare-specific constraint items (SI-09, SI-13).

03

SI-10 is the highest-risk domain authenticity flag in this set. The "reasonably should have known" standard is the kind of legal nuance that feels minor until you see a covered entity defend a compliance failure in front of OCR. Training that teaches the wrong trigger will produce systematic non-compliance at MRHN. This annotation must survive into decomposition unchanged.

04

When taxonomy assignments surprise me, I write the reason in the rationale column rather than just recording the level. The reason is what matters for decomposition. "Analysis" without a rationale could be downgraded by a Module 2 designer who thinks the item looks straightforward. The rationale explains why the population makes a task harder than it appears.

05

SI-06 is the taxonomy judgment I am most confident will be challenged in review. It looks like a procedure lookup -- apply the escalation criteria and read the answer. But healthcare IT professionals under pressure do not apply criteria; they default to escalation. The Analysis assignment is not about the procedure; it is about the behavioral pattern this population brings to it. The assessment must surface that pattern, not just test the criteria.

06

The calibration annotation layer is the most important handoff document in Phase 1. Everything before it -- the scope list, the bucket map, the balance check -- built the structure. The calibration layer tells the Module 2 designer what each item actually requires from learners. Without it, decomposition defaults to Application level for every item and misses the five items where that default is wrong.
