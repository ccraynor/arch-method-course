Bucketing Scope Items: Reading 16 Items and Organizing by Workflow Logic

Step 2
Scenario: Your 16 MRHN scope items (SI-01 through SI-16) are declared and documented. Watch an expert read the full set and organize them into four named buckets. The expert uses workflow sequence as the organizing axis, not subject-matter topic.

You could sort these 16 items by topic in a few minutes. So why does the expert refuse to group anything until every item has been read?

01

Read All 16 Scope Items Before Assigning Any to a Bucket

Before grouping a single item, I read all 16 as a set. The first pass is observational: I am not making decisions yet. I am building a picture of the full scope so that when I do group items, I am grouping within the whole, not organizing partial information into categories that will need to be restructured when the rest of the set appears.

The complete MRHN scope item set, carrying forward from Step 1.5:

ID Scope Item
SI-01 IR-2024 Tier 1 protocol execution: independent, no escalation required
SI-02 IR-2024 Tier 2 protocol execution: within role authority
SI-03 System isolation decisions: role-bounded, no supervisor required
SI-04 Real-time SIEM event log documentation during active incident
SI-05 Clinical leadership communication using Meridian approved templates
SI-06 Escalation protocol: recognizing when a decision requires supervisor involvement
SI-07 Post-incident OCR-compliant documentation and breach reporting procedure
SI-08 Threat classification: Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 identification from SIEM alerts
SI-09 Evidence preservation: maintaining chain of custody during active incident response
SI-10 HIPAA breach notification timeline: managing the 72-hour OCR reporting window

After reading the full set, two things are clear: (1) these items span the entire incident response lifecycle, and (2) many of them only make sense in relation to each other: SI-16 (reclassification) leads to SI-06 (escalation), which changes the protocol from SI-01 to SI-02. That sequencing is the organizing logic I will use for bucketing.

02

Choose the Organizing Axis: Workflow Sequence, Not Topic Familiarity

The first instinct when bucketing content is to group by topic: "all the documentation items," "all the protocol items," "all the communication items." That instinct produces structurally weak architecture. Here is the contrast:

Topic-Based Grouping: Avoid
What this produces:
  • Protocol Knowledge (SI-01, SI-02, SI-08, SI-16)
  • Technical Actions (SI-03, SI-09, SI-11, SI-12)
  • Communication (SI-05, SI-06, SI-15)
  • Documentation (SI-04, SI-07, SI-10, SI-13, SI-14)
Workflow-Based Grouping: Preferred
What this produces:
  • Threat Recognition and Triage (5 items)
  • Containment and Control (4 items)
  • Active Response Execution (4 items)
  • Post-Incident Close-Out (3 items)

The problem with topic-based grouping is structural: it puts SI-04 (real-time SIEM documentation) and SI-07 (post-incident OCR documentation) in the same bucket because they are both documentation tasks. But SI-04 happens during active containment and SI-07 happens after the incident is closed. A course organized by topic would require learners to learn both documentation behaviors together and then apply them at different points in the workflow: exactly the integration gap that stress-testing will later flag.

Workflow-based grouping puts every scope item in the phase of the response cycle where it actually occurs. Learners practice each bucket in the sequence they will execute it, with prerequisites established before they are needed. The architecture mirrors the work.

03

Bucket 1: Threat Recognition and Triage

The first workflow phase is the entry gate to everything that follows. Before any containment action can be taken, a responder must classify the incident, determine which protocol applies, and confirm whether they are authorized to execute it alone. All five items in Bucket 1 answer the same question: what is this incident and am I the right person to handle it?

1 Threat Recognition and Triage
5 items
Grouping rationale All five items are decisions that must be resolved in the first minutes of an incident, before any physical or technical response action begins. They share a common prerequisite structure: you cannot execute Tier 1 protocol (SI-01) without first classifying the threat (SI-08), and you cannot stay on Tier 1 if reclassification occurs (SI-16), which triggers the escalation check (SI-06) and potentially upgrades the response to Tier 2 (SI-02).
SI-08
Threat classification: Tier 1 vs. Tier 2 from SIEM alerts Entry point of triage; every subsequent decision depends on this call
SI-01
IR-2024 Tier 1 protocol execution: independent, no escalation Activated immediately after Tier 1 classification; role-authorized solo execution
SI-16
Incident severity reclassification: upgrading Tier 1 to Tier 2 mid-response Occurs during triage when initial classification was wrong or conditions changed
SI-06
Escalation protocol: recognizing when a decision requires supervisor involvement Triggered by reclassification (SI-16) or initial Tier 2 determination; precedes Tier 2 execution
SI-02
IR-2024 Tier 2 protocol execution: within role authority Activated after escalation check confirms Tier 2 and role authorization; still role-bounded (PC-3)
04

Bucket 2: Containment and Control

Once the incident is classified and the appropriate protocol is activated, the responder moves into containment: stopping the spread, isolating affected systems, preserving evidence, and notifying affected external parties. All four items in Bucket 2 are physical or operational actions executed in the containment window, which begins immediately after triage and ends when active spread is stopped.

2 Containment and Control
4 items
Grouping rationale All four items share a critical constraint: PC-3 (role-bounded authority). Each action must be executable within the responder's role without supervisor sign-off. The grouping is not just workflow position: it is constraint coherence. Every item in this bucket is governed by the same binding rule.
SI-03
System isolation decisions: role-bounded, no supervisor required First containment action; must be executable without delay to limit spread
SI-11
Network segmentation decisions: isolating affected systems to limit lateral spread Follows or runs concurrent with system isolation; broader scope than SI-03
SI-09
Evidence preservation: maintaining chain of custody during active incident response Must begin during containment to be legally defensible; cannot be retroactive
SI-15
Vendor and third-party system notification during active incident External containment action; runs concurrently with internal isolation when third-party systems are affected
05

Bucket 3: Active Response Execution

While containment is ongoing, and after active spread is stopped but before the incident is formally closed, a responder carries concurrent obligations: real-time documentation, communication upward to clinical leadership, managing the regulatory notification clock, and recording any deviations from the written protocol. These four items happen while the incident is still active.

The placement of SI-10 (HIPAA breach notification timeline) here rather than in post-incident close-out requires explicit rationale. The 72-hour OCR notification clock starts at the point of discovery, not at the point of containment. Managing that clock is a live obligation during active response, not a post-closure task.

Notice the surprise: breach notification lands in active response, not close-out, because the 72-hour clock starts the moment you discover the incident.
3 Active Response Execution
4 items
Grouping rationale All four items are concurrent obligations during active incident response. None can be deferred to post-incident close-out without legal or operational consequence. They are also the highest-constraint items in the scope set: SI-04 is governed by PC-4, SI-05 by PC-5, SI-10 by RC-1 and RC-3, and SI-14 directly by RC-2 (IR-2024 compliance).
SI-04
Real-time SIEM event log documentation during active incident Continuous obligation from incident start; log accuracy depends on real-time entry, not memory
SI-05
Clinical leadership communication using Meridian approved templates Upward communication obligation; triggered once incident is confirmed, before close-out
SI-10
HIPAA breach notification timeline: managing the 72-hour OCR reporting window Clock starts at discovery; placed here because tracking begins during active response, not after closure
SI-14
IR-2024 protocol deviation documentation: recording when steps cannot be followed as written Must be recorded as deviations occur, not reconstructed after; binds to RC-2 compliance
06

Bucket 4: Post-Incident Close-Out

After containment is complete and the active incident is formally closed, three obligations remain. They share a common prerequisite: containment must be verified before any of them begin. SI-07 and SI-13 are documentation; SI-12 is a verification action that unlocks system restoration. This bucket is the smallest in the set, which requires a documented rationale and a Step 3 balance check.

4 Post-Incident Close-Out
3 items
Grouping rationale All three items require containment to be confirmed before they begin. SI-12 (recovery verification) is the physical gate: you cannot restore systems to production without confirming integrity. SI-07 (OCR documentation) and SI-13 (lessons learned) both depend on having a complete incident record to draw from, which cannot exist until the incident is closed and all active response documentation is consolidated.
SI-12
Recovery verification: confirming system integrity before restoring to production Technical gate action; must be completed before any system restoration; opens the close-out phase
SI-07
Post-incident OCR-compliant documentation and breach reporting procedure Formal regulatory documentation; requires complete incident record; governed by RC-1 and RC-3
SI-13
Post-incident lessons learned: documenting what happened and what to change Organizational learning output; depends on full incident record and completed close-out documentation
Step 3 balance flag: Bucket 4 contains 3 items: the smallest bucket in the set. This does not require merging with Bucket 3. The items share a distinct prerequisite (containment confirmation) that separates them clearly from active execution. However, the balance evaluation in Step 3 will confirm whether 3 items provide sufficient learning content to justify a standalone bucket against the project constraints.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

The most common bucketing error is starting before you have seen the full set. The first five items look like a natural group until you read item 16 and realize the sequencing logic is different than you assumed. First pass: read everything. Zero decisions.

02

Topic-based grouping feels natural because it matches how subject-matter experts think about their field. But learners are not being trained to think about the field: they are being trained to perform in the field. Those two things require different organizational logic.

03

Bucket 1 has 5 items: the largest bucket. I am not splitting it to achieve numerical balance. The 5 items form an unbroken decision chain (classify, activate, reclassify, escalate, re-activate). Splitting that chain would teach the decision sequence in two separate buckets, which breaks the prerequisite logic.

04

The signal that these four belong together is not just workflow position: it is that they are all governed by PC-3 (role-bounded authority). When scope items share a governing constraint, that constraint usually defines the learning challenge for the bucket. Here: can you identify and execute the right containment action within your authority, without waiting for supervisor confirmation?

05

SI-10 is the most debated placement. Post-incident designers want to put it in close-out because breach notification "happens after the incident." But the 72-hour clock starts at discovery, not at closure. A course that teaches notification timing only in the post-incident module will produce learners who don't think about the clock until it is almost out of time.

06

Three items does not automatically mean the bucket is too thin. The test is not "how many items" but "do these items require meaningful learning content and do they have a coherent prerequisite structure?" SI-12, SI-07, and SI-13 all pass that test. Step 3 will confirm whether the instructional content they generate fits within the project constraints.
