Assumptions Under Pressure: What the Audit Work Revealed

Reflection Prompt

Every practitioner enters decomposition with working assumptions. You assume how many components a scope item generates. You assume whether components can be sized on their own. You assume whether tracing is a formality or a real quality check. This lesson put those assumptions under pressure you could see. Before you close Module 1, name one and account for what happened to it.

  1. The assumption: Identify one assumption you held about decomposition before working through this lesson. It can be about component sizing, traceability, scope coverage, or audit rigor. State it precisely enough that another practitioner would recognize it as a real position, not a vague intuition.
  2. What the work revealed: Did the optimization decisions or the trace audit challenge or confirm that assumption? Name the specific moment, a verdict, a correction, a calibration note, that made the assumption visible. Then state what, if anything, changes in your approach going forward.

Writing Guidance

  • Name the assumption precisely, not "I thought decomposition was hard" but "I assumed every scope item maps to two components at matched taxonomy levels."
  • Connect your assumption to a specific item from Pages 2-4: which component, verdict, or trace result made the assumption visible?
  • Confirmed assumptions are as informative as challenged ones. If the work confirmed what you expected, explain what evidence confirmed it and what that does to your confidence.
  • State a concrete change: not "I will be more careful" but "I will run the Q1 directional test before assigning a count, not after."
