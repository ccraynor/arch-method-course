Lesson 4

Completing the Formal MRHN Bucket Map: Three-Component Artifact Assembly

Step 3
Scenario: Your four-bucket MRHN structure is balance-checked and MRHN-D-002 is on record. Watch an expert assemble the three-component formal artifact. It closes Step 3 and hands off a governed scope document to the Step 4 calibration process.

You already made every bucket decision back in Lesson 3. So why does turning those decisions into a formal artifact still take real work?

01

Why the Formal Artifact Has Three Components

The bucket map produced in Steps 2 and 3 exists as decisions at this point: a structure that lives in a spreadsheet, a whiteboard, or the analysis from Lesson 3. The formal artifact converts those decisions into a governed document that can be reviewed, audited, and handed off. Three components are required because each one answers a different accountability question.

Component 1, Scope Item Table: Answers "what is in scope and where does each item belong?" Every one of the 16 SI items must appear with its bucket assignment and the workflow rationale that justifies that assignment. The rationale is what makes this a governed artifact rather than a list.

Component 2, Balance Check Summary: Answers "has this structure been evaluated against the four balance criteria?" The summary condenses the full analysis from Step 3 into a compact record, one row per bucket, one cell per criterion. Any flags are noted with their resolution.

Component 3, Governance Record MRHN-D-002: Answers "who made this decision, against what constraints, and what were the risks?" This is the legal and audit record. It is the most important component for stakeholder review and for anyone who needs to understand why the architecture is structured this way.

I assemble these in order, table first, summary second, governance record third, because each component draws on the previous one. The table is the input to the balance check; the balance check feeds the rationale in the governance record.

02

Component 1: Scope Item Table

The table has three columns: SI identifier, item name with a brief description, and workflow rationale. The rationale column is the governed element: it must state why the item belongs in this bucket based on workflow position, not topic similarity. Items are grouped by bucket with a divider row showing the bucket name.

Component 2: Balance Check Summary

The balance check summary condenses the full Step 3 analysis into a single reference table. The intent is not to re-run the analysis; it is to make the outcome of the analysis visible at a glance. Each cell shows the verdict for that criterion and bucket. Flags include a brief resolution note. This table is what a reviewer reads to confirm the architecture was evaluated before decomposition began.

04

Component 3: Governance Record MRHN-D-002

The governance record draws directly from the scope item table (which produced the decision) and the balance check summary (which confirmed it). Each of the five fields is completed from those two sources. The only field that requires fresh thinking is Field 4 (Risk Note), which is where I capture what could go wrong with this decision going forward.

3 Governance Record: MRHN-D-002
Decision Record: MRHN-D-002: Bucket Map Balance Check
1 Decision Statement
The MRHN course architecture will proceed with four buckets in the sequence established in Step 2: Threat Recognition and Triage (5 items), Containment and Control (4 items), Active Response Execution (4 items), and Post-Incident Close-Out (3 items). All 16 scope items are assigned. No re-bucketing is required. The balance check is complete.
2 Constraint Reference
LC-1 (4-hour SME access cap); LC-3 and LC-4 (SCORM self-paced, 90-day window); PC-3 (role-bounded authority, Buckets 2 and 1); PC-4 (documentation authority, SI-04); PC-5 (communication authority, SI-05); RC-1, RC-3 (OCR compliance, Buckets 3 and 4); RC-2 (IR-2024 audit, SI-14)
3 Rationale
All four buckets satisfy constraint compliance, appropriate item volume, instructional coherence, and sequence position. Bucket 1 carries five items justified by the linear decision chain structure: sequential cognitive load is appropriate for a triage workflow. Bucket 4 carries three items; the thin-bucket test confirms sufficient instructional substance, a distinct containment-confirmation prerequisite, constraint compliance within the LC-1 budget, and a merge test that shows combining with Bucket 3 would eliminate a structurally critical temporal boundary between concurrent-response and post-closure obligations.
4 Risk Note
Risk 1 (SME allocation): Buckets 1 and 3 both carry high constraint density. If SME time is consumed disproportionately on these two buckets, Bucket 4 item SI-07 (OCR breach documentation) may receive insufficient expert review before the 90-day delivery window closes. Mitigation: establish SME allocation priorities in the project plan before decomposition begins in Module 2.

Risk 2 (concurrency instruction): Bucket 3 items SI-04 and SI-10 begin concurrently with Bucket 2. If Module 2 instruction treats Buckets 2 and 3 as strictly sequential, learners may not develop real-time multi-obligation management skills. Mitigation: flag this concurrency explicitly during Module 2 decomposition of SI-04 and SI-10.
5 Checkpoint Linkage
Gate 1.TG.1 (Module 1 Transition Gate) will verify that all four buckets are constraint-compliant and that MRHN-D-002 is complete. Module 3 Outcome Mapping will confirm that instructional content within each bucket prepares learners for the specific performance requirements of that workflow phase. Step 4 Domain Calibration (this lesson, Screen 3) will evaluate each scope item for domain authenticity and taxonomy level before decomposition begins.
05

Review for Completeness Before Finalizing

Before assigning the artifact a version number, I run a completeness check. This takes two minutes and prevents the most common errors: a missing scope item, an empty governance field, or a flag that was noted in the balance summary but not carried into the risk note.

Completeness Check: MRHN_BucketMap_v1
All 16 scope items (SI-01 through SI-16) are present in the Scope Item Table with bucket assignments and workflow rationale.
All four buckets have a verdict for all four balance criteria in the Balance Check Summary. Flags include resolution notes.
Governance record MRHN-D-002 has all five fields completed. No field is blank or contains placeholder text.
Both flags from the Balance Check Summary (Bucket 3 constraint density, Bucket 4 item volume) appear in the Risk Note field of MRHN-D-002.
Checkpoint Linkage field references Gate 1.TG.1 and Module 3 Outcome Mapping, the two downstream checkpoints that validate this artifact.
Step 4 calibration (Domain Authenticity and Taxonomy) is named in Checkpoint Linkage as the immediate next step. Handoff is explicit.

All six checks pass. The artifact is ready for version assignment.

06

Assign Version, Apply Naming Convention, and Hand Off to Step 4

The artifact naming convention established in Step 1.5 governs the final artifact name. This is version 1 because no calibration has been applied yet. If Step 4 calibration produces scope adjustments, the bucket map is incremented to v2 and a new governance record is added: it is not modified in place.

Artifact Complete: Step 3 Closed MRHN_BucketMap_v1_[Date] Version 1: initial balance-checked bucket map. Three components: Scope Item Table (16 items), Balance Check Summary (4 buckets, 4 criteria), Governance Record MRHN-D-002 (5 fields). Next step: Hand off to Step 4 Domain Calibration. If calibration produces scope adjustments, increment to MRHN_BucketMap_v2_[Date] and add MRHN-D-003.

The version note matters: v1 is what entered calibration. The calibrated output is a different artifact: MRHN_CalibratedScopeMap_v1. These are separate documents. The bucket map record remains unchanged as the pre-calibration baseline. Any reviewer who wants to see what the architecture looked like before Step 4 adjustments reads MRHN_BucketMap_v1; any reviewer who wants to see the calibrated scope reads MRHN_CalibratedScopeMap_v1.

Expert Think-Aloud

01

The three-component structure exists because an artifact without rationale is not governed: it is a list. The scope item table without a balance summary could be a first draft. The balance summary without a governance record is an analysis without an owner. All three are required for the document to function as an audit-ready artifact.

02

The workflow rationale column is where designers most often take shortcuts. Writing "belongs in active response" is not a rationale: it restates the assignment. A rationale names a workflow position, a dependency, or a governing constraint. The test: could a reviewer reconstruct the bucketing decision from the rationale alone, without seeing the bucket name? If yes, the rationale is adequate.

03

Flags belong in the summary even when resolved. A reviewer who sees a clean row of checkmarks assumes either that everything passed trivially or that problems were not noticed. The flag for Bucket 4 item volume, with the note that the thin-bucket test resolved it, shows that the analysis was rigorous, not that it was easy.

04

Field 4 (Risk Note) is the field most commonly left incomplete. Designers who are satisfied with their architecture tend to write "none" or leave it blank. This is the wrong instinct: a decision that carries no risk is either very simple or not examined closely enough. Both risks recorded here (SME allocation skew, concurrency instruction gap) are genuine and will need to be addressed in Module 2.

05

The completeness check takes two minutes and prevents one of the most common handoff errors: a flag that appeared in the analysis but was not carried into the governance record. The person who runs Step 4 calibration will read the governance record, not the working notes. If a risk is not in Field 4, it will not be considered.

06

Keeping v1 and v2 as separate documents is deliberate. It is tempting to update in place: just edit the bucket map when calibration changes something. But then the pre-calibration record is lost. In a real project, a stakeholder may want to understand what changed in Step 4 and why. Two documents with two version numbers make that comparison trivial.
