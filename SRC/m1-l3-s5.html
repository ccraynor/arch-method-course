Lesson Reflection

Reflection Prompt

Which calibration finding from this lesson would most change how you approach scope definition in your own design practice, and why? Be specific about what you would do differently and what that change would protect against.

Optional: Use a structured approach

If you prefer a guided approach, work through these four prompts in sequence. You can write your response as prose or follow the structure exactly.

  1. Name the finding. Which specific calibration result surprised you most, a domain authenticity flag, a taxonomy level that differed from your initial expectation, or the reasoning behind one of the population-specific adjustments?
  2. Name the default. What would you have done without this finding? Describe the content or assessment you would have built had you treated the item as generic or at the taxonomy level you initially expected.
  3. Name the correction. What specifically would you do differently now? Not in the MRHN scenario, in your own current or recent design work. Be concrete: what would change in your scope list, your taxonomy assignments, or your calibration notes?
  4. Name the protection. What downstream error does this change prevent? Who or what is protected, the learner, the organization, the compliance record, the course design itself?
Reference: All Calibration Findings from This Lesson

Domain Authenticity Flags

  • SI-10 (HIPAA clock): Clock triggers at "reasonably should have known" (45 CFR 164.404), not forensic confirmation. Generic courses teach the wrong trigger.
  • SI-07 (OCR documentation): Requires 8 specific data elements under 45 CFR 164.408. "File a report" hides significant procedural complexity.
  • SI-13 (Lessons learned): Document may be discoverable in litigation. Generic retrospective framing creates legal exposure at MRHN.
  • SI-14 (Deviation documentation): RC-2 requires specific justification language, not notation only. Deviation vs. adaptation is a taught distinction.
  • SI-09 (Network segmentation): MRHN multi-site topology requires evaluating EHR, imaging, and billing interdependencies before segmentation. Clinical care availability changes the task.

Taxonomy Adjustments (this learner population)

  • SI-10: Application elevated to Analysis. "Reasonably should have known" is legal reasoning under ambiguous facts, not timer management.
  • SI-13: Application elevated to Evaluation. Judging what to document given legal exposure: no prescribed answer.
  • SI-09: Application elevated to Analysis. Clinical interdependency evaluation required before applying segmentation procedure.
  • SI-08: Analysis (not Application). Distinguishing security incidents from system failures requires reasoning without a practiced pattern library.
  • SI-06: Analysis (not Application). Healthcare IT professionals default to escalation; "protocol" language masks a genuine capability evaluation judgment.
