Lesson 1

Declaring Locked Scope for Meridian

Purpose

You are about to write your first scope boundary statement, the first output of Step 0. This is how you turn a messy client brief into clear, binding constraints. Get this right, and you produce a declaration precise enough to hand a client with confidence.

Instructions

  1. Review the Meridian brief on Screen 2 if needed.
  2. Complete all three sections of the scope boundary statement below.
  3. Select Check My Work when you are ready.

Expected Evidence

You produce a three-part scope boundary statement precise enough to present to Priya Okonkwo. It should guide your first SME meeting without any extra explanation.

Include the target learner population (with role specificity), the performance domain (reference IR-2024 by name), and the delivery parameters.

Identify at least two categories of content, audiences, or activities that fall outside this course's boundaries. Draw from the brief's specificity about who and what is in scope.

In 2 to 3 sentences, explain which specific constraints from the Meridian brief shaped these boundaries.

How confident are you in your answers?

Success Criteria

  • Your in-scope statement names the specific IT roles and references IR-2024 by name, not in general terms.
  • You identify at least two categories of content that fall outside scope.
  • Your rationale ties each boundary to a specific constraint from the brief, such as the SME time limit or the OCR timeline.

What your response addressed:

Expert Scope Boundary Statement

Your Development

What your response reveals: you are learning to treat scope boundaries as binding agreements, not loose descriptions. What to carry forward: name what falls outside the course as clearly as what falls inside it, because both protect your design.
